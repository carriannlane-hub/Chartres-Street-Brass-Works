The Art of Dent Removal: Why Every Mark Tells a Story

Posted on February 28, 2025 by Paul Varnado

Forty years I've been working brass, and I still get excited when a really challenging piece walks through my door. Today's project was a 1940s Selmer trumpet with more character than a French Quarter jazz funeral.

The owner, a young cat named Marcus, inherited it from his great-uncle who played in the Preservation Hall band back in the day. The bell had three major dents and what looked like water damage around the third valve slide. But here's the thing about old brass - the metal remembers.

I started with the biggest dent, right on the bell's sweet spot. Used my smallest ball peen and worked from the inside, tapping in a spiral pattern. The metal wanted to cooperate - good brass always does. As I worked, I could almost hear the music this horn had made over the decades.

Marcus told me his uncle used to say the trumpet had a secret hidden inside. Family legend, probably, but I've learned not to dismiss such things entirely. New Orleans instruments have seen more than their share of mysteries.

Three hours later, the dents were gone and the trumpet was singing again. Sweet, clear tone with that warm brass resonance that only comes with age and proper care. Marcus tested it right there in the shop - played a few bars of "Mood Indigo" that would have made his great-uncle proud.

Late Night Sessions: When the Music Calls

Posted on May 15, 2025 by Paul Varnado

The Quarter gets a different personality after midnight. The tourist crowds thin out, the street musicians pack up, and the real New Orleans shows itself. That's when I do my best thinking and, often, my most important work.

Last Tuesday, around 11 PM, I was just closing up when this young woman knocked on my door. Rain-soaked and clutching a case like her life depended on it. She said her name was Claire Beauregard, and she needed help with her grandmother's cornet - had a gig the next morning and the second valve was stuck solid.

I invited her upstairs to the workshop. The cornet was a beauty - 1920s Conn, silver-plated with intricate engravings. But something was off about the second valve casing. There were tiny scratches around the edges, like someone had been trying to pry it open.

While I worked on freeing the valve, Claire told me about her grandmother, Celeste Beauregard, who used to play in the women's jazz ensembles during the '40s. "She always said this cornet held our family's fortune," Claire laughed nervously. "I thought she was just being dramatic."

When the valve finally came free, something rattled inside the tubing. A small roll of papers had been stuffed into the third valve slide. Claire went pale when she saw them - looked like old property deeds with some very familiar French Quarter addresses.

I cleaned and oiled the valves while Claire stared at those papers. By the time I finished, the cornet was playing sweeter than honey. But Claire seemed more interested in those documents than the repair. She paid me, thanked me quickly, and disappeared back into the rainy night.

Haven't seen her since, but I keep thinking about those papers. In this city, family secrets have a way of surfacing when you least expect them.

A Once-in-a-Lifetime Restoration: The Marseille Zodiac

Posted on August 8, 2025 by Paul Varnado

Thirty-five years in this business, and I thought I'd seen every rare brass instrument that existed. Then last month, Alcide Beaudreaux walked into my shop carrying something that made my heart skip a beat - an 1847 Marseille "Phoenix" trumpet with hand-engraved zodiac symbols around the valve casings.

Antoine Marseille only made twelve of these before his Lyon workshop burned down in late 1847. The few that survived the fire earned the nickname "Phoenix" - instruments that literally rose from the ashes. This particular trumpet, serial #7, had been in Alcide's family for generations, passed down from his great-great-grandfather who brought it to Louisiana in the 1890s.

The trumpet was in rough shape when Alcide brought it to me. The revolutionary double-wall bell construction was intact, thank God, but decades of New Orleans humidity had taken their toll. The mother-of-pearl valve inlays were clouded, the gold leaf accents on the bell were tarnished beyond recognition, and the secret 85% copper, 15% silver alloy had developed a patina that obscured Marseille's exquisite craftsmanship.

But the zodiac engravings - Lord, they were still magnificent. Each symbol carved with the precision that made Marseille legendary. Aries on the first valve, Taurus on the second, Gemini on the third. The detail work was so fine you could see individual stars within the constellation patterns.

I spent three weeks on that restoration, working with methods I'd researched specifically for 19th-century French brass. The valve work alone took five days - those original nickel-silver mechanisms needed the gentlest touch. When I finally got it reassembled and Alcide played the first notes, that "liquid gold" tone that musicians write about filled my workshop. Pure magic.

Alcide was over the moon when he picked it up. He mentioned something about getting it appraised for insurance purposes, given its incredible rarity. Only four of these Marseille trumpets are known to exist worldwide. An instrument like this... well, let's just say it represents a significant family asset that deserves proper protection.

I was honored to work on such a historically significant piece. Some restorations pay the bills, but others remind you why you fell in love with this craft in the first place. The Marseille Zodiac was definitely the latter.
